What problem is Model Integrity Review designed to address?
MIR addresses the gap between technically sophisticated models and the clarity required for decision-making, review, audit, or external scrutiny. Many models are sound but insufficiently documented, difficult to explain, or fragile under questioning. MIR focuses on that structural and documentation layer.
How is this different from validation, peer review, or quality assurance?
Validation and peer review assess correctness within a technical domain. Quality assurance often checks process compliance. MIR does neither. It examines whether the model, as documented, is internally coherent, transparent in its assumptions, and defensible as a decision artifact — independent of whether it is “right.”
Does a Model Integrity Review imply endorsement or approval?
No. MIR does not endorse conclusions, certify outcomes, or approve decisions. The review is advisory and descriptive. Its purpose is to surface documentation and structural risks so decision-makers understand where clarity exists and where it does not.
What materials does the review rely on?
Reviews are based solely on materials provided by the client, such as reports, technical memoranda, appendices, spreadsheets, or excerpts. MIR does not run models, collect new data, or infer behavior beyond what is documented.
What kinds of issues typically emerge in a review?
Common observations include undocumented assumptions, unclear input–output traceability, inconsistent terminology, ambiguous normalization or scaling choices, and gaps between narrative descriptions and actual structure. These are framed as documentation or governance risks, not technical errors.
Who typically requests a Model Integrity Review?
Requests most often come from senior managers, consultants, research leads, or governance functions responsible for explaining or standing behind a model they did not personally author.
How should the review be used after delivery?
The review memorandum is intended to support internal understanding, documentation improvement, and informed discussion. It may be shared internally or with reviewers at the client’s discretion, with the understanding that it is not a certification or validation document.
Are engagements confidential?
Yes. All client materials are treated as confidential. MIR does not disclose client information or reuse materials across engagements. Internal analytical aids used during review are not client-facing.
When is a Model Integrity Review not appropriate?
MIR is not appropriate when validation, regulatory sign-off, litigation support, or technical redesign is required. In such cases, specialized domain or legal services are more appropriate.
